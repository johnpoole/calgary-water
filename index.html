How “Risk” is calculated

Risk is computed per main as a combination of Probability of Failure (PoF) and Consequence of Failure (CoF). The map can also apply a major-road uplift and then uses a visual binning step to color the Risk overlay.

1) Base PoF (1–4)

  • Starts from a material-based likelihood (e.g. PVC/PE lower, DI/Steel mid, CI/AC higher).
  • Adjusts for age/vintage (e.g. CI & AC older than 50 years are elevated; AC near/over 50 is treated as approaching end-of-life; PCCP/PCI manufactured 1972–1978 is highest, and other PCCP/PCI in 1970–1980 is elevated).
  • Adjusts for status indicators that suggest abandoned/out-of-service.

2) Base CoF (1–4)

  • Primarily driven by diameter (small = lower consequence, large = higher).
  • Material hints: PCCP/PCI tends to very high consequence; large steel can be high; copper tends low.
  • Very long segments get a small bump.

3) Risk class (1–4)

  • Computed from the PoF×CoF product (higher product → higher risk class).

4) Major-road uplift

  • If a main is close to a classified major road (precomputed offline), an uplift (e.g. +0.5 to +2.0) is added to the PoF float score for eligible materials (and old PVC).

5) Risk overlay colors (visual binning)

  • The Risk overlay uses 6 colors and bins mains by rank so that about 90% fall in bin 1, then 90% of what remains in bin 2, etc. (This is to keep contrast when many mains share similar scores.)
  • This binning affects the map colors only; the underlying PoF/CoF and risk class values remain the same.
